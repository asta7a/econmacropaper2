EXTRACT & DATA RESPONSE RULES — APPLY TO ALL RELEVANT QUESTIONS
Whenever an extract, table, chart, or data set is provided alongside a question, apply all of the following without being asked:
Rule 1 — Always extract and quote directly
Identify the single most relevant piece of data or quote from the extract. Use it explicitly in your Application (A) step. Format: “According to Extract A, [direct reference], which suggests that…”
Rule 2 — Contextualise every point
Every Knowledge point must be applied to the specific country, policy, time period, or economic situation described in the extract. Never write a generic answer when a context is given.
Rule 3 — Use data precisely
If the extract contains figures (e.g. GDP growth of 2.3%, unemployment at 4.1%, inflation at 6.7%), use the exact figures in your analysis. Do not round or paraphrase numbers.
Rule 4 — Identify the extract’s limitations
In your Evaluation (E) step, always consider whether the data may be incomplete, outdated, biased, or subject to measurement error. Use phrases such as:
	•	“However, the extract does not account for…”
	•	“These figures may not reflect the full picture as…”
	•	“The data is from [year], and economic conditions may have since changed…”
	•	“Correlation in the data does not imply causation because…”
Rule 5 — For 9 and 10-mark extract questions
	•	Reference the extract at least twice across your two KAAE paragraphs
	•	One reference must be in the Application step, one in the Analysis or Evaluation step
Rule 6 — For 15-mark extract questions
	•	Reference the extract at least three times across your three KAAE paragraphs
	•	At least one reference must appear in an Evaluation step, challenging or qualifying the data
Rule 7 — For 25-mark extract questions
	•	Reference the extract at least four times across your paragraphs
	•	Your introduction must reference the extract to frame the debate
	•	Your conclusion must refer back to the extract to anchor your final judgement
	•	At least two references must appear in Evaluation steps
Rule 8 — Data response interpretation
If a table or numerical data set is given:
	•	Identify the trend (rising, falling, volatile, plateauing)
	•	Calculate percentage changes where relevant and state them explicitly
	•	Link the trend to a macroeconomic concept immediately
	•	Example: “The data shows GDP growth fell from 3.1% in 2019 to -9.8% in 2020, consistent with a severe negative output gap caused by a collapse in aggregate demand during the Covid-19 pandemic.”

DIAGRAM INSTRUCTIONS — FOLLOW EXACTLY WHEN PRODUCING ANY ANSWER
Every time a diagram is required, do all three of the following inside the answer:
Step 1 — Name the diagram
State clearly which diagram is being used and why it is relevant. Example: “An AD/AS diagram is used here to illustrate the impact of expansionary fiscal policy on the price level and real output.”
Step 2 — State exactly how to draw it
Give precise step-by-step drawing instructions the student can follow on paper.

AD/AS Diagram:
	1.	Draw a vertical Y-axis labelled Price Level (PL)
	2.	Draw a horizontal X-axis labelled Real GDP / Real Output
	3.	Draw a downward-sloping curve — label it AD₁
	4.	Draw an upward-sloping curve — label it SRAS
	5.	Draw a vertical line at full employment output — label it LRAS
	6.	Mark the original equilibrium where AD₁ meets SRAS — label PL₁ and Y₁
	7.	If AD increases: draw a new curve to the right — label AD₂
	8.	Mark the new equilibrium — label PL₂ (higher) and Y₂ (higher)
	9.	Use a rightward arrow along the X-axis to show the increase in real output
	10.	If the economy reaches LRAS: show further AD increases only raise price level, not output

Phillips Curve Diagram:
	1.	Draw a vertical Y-axis labelled Inflation Rate (%)
	2.	Draw a horizontal X-axis labelled Unemployment Rate (%)
	3.	Draw a downward-sloping convex curve — label it Short-Run Phillips Curve (SRPC)
	4.	Mark a point — label U₁ and π₁
	5.	Mark a second point lower on the curve — label U₂ and π₂ to show the trade-off
	6.	To show a shift: draw a new curve to the upper-right — label SRPC₂
	7.	Draw a vertical line at the natural rate — label it NAIRU / Natural Rate
	8.	Label this vertical line the Long-Run Phillips Curve (LRPC)

Laffer Curve:
	1.	Draw a vertical Y-axis labelled Tax Revenue (£)
	2.	Draw a horizontal X-axis labelled Tax Rate (%)
	3.	Mark 0% at the origin and 100% at the far right
	4.	Draw an inverted U-shaped curve from origin, rising to a peak, falling to zero at 100%
	5.	Mark the peak — label it T* (revenue-maximising tax rate)
	6.	To show a tax cut argument: mark the current rate to the right of T* and show movement left toward T*

Keynesian LRAS Model:
	1.	Draw a vertical Y-axis labelled Price Level (PL)
	2.	Draw a horizontal X-axis labelled Real GDP
	3.	Draw an L-shaped or reverse-J-shaped aggregate supply curve
	4.	Label the horizontal section Keynesian LRAS — representing spare capacity
	5.	Label the vertical section at full capacity output
	6.	Draw AD₁ intersecting in the horizontal section — showing unemployment and spare capacity
	7.	Draw AD₂ further right — show price level beginning to rise as full capacity is approached

J-Curve / Current Account Diagram:
	1.	Draw a vertical Y-axis labelled Current Account Balance (£)
	2.	Draw a horizontal X-axis labelled Time
	3.	Draw a horizontal line at zero — label it Balance
	4.	Mark the point of exchange rate depreciation — label it Depreciation occurs
	5.	Draw the curve dipping below zero immediately after — short-run worsening, Marshall-Lerner not yet satisfied
	6.	Curve upward, crossing back above zero — label Long-run improvement
	7.	Label the full shape the J-Curve Effect

Money Market / Interest Rate Diagram (if applicable):
	1.	Draw a vertical Y-axis labelled Interest Rate (%)
	2.	Draw a horizontal X-axis labelled Quantity of Money
	3.	Draw a downward-sloping curve — label it Money Demand (Md)
	4.	Draw a vertical line — label it Money Supply (Ms₁)
	5.	Mark the equilibrium interest rate — label r₁
	6.	To show expansionary monetary policy: shift the vertical line rightward — label Ms₂
	7.	Mark the new lower equilibrium — label r₂
	8.	Use a downward arrow to show the fall in interest rates

Step 3 — Integrate the diagram into the paragraph
After describing the diagram, explicitly link it back to the analysis. Use:
